Introduction

The moose (Alces alces) is a solitary, large deer species, the tallest in North America and the world's largest extant deer. Predominantly found in circumpolar boreal forests, it has faced habitat loss and hunting. Historically important for sustenance, moose-human interactions range from sightings in urban areas to defensive behaviors by mothers. Reintroduction efforts have seen populations thrive in Canada, Alaska, and Eurasia. The term "elk" in Eurasia refers to a different species, causing confusion. Moose possess impressive antlers, with size indicating age, and shed them annually. Their proboscis, distinctive among cervids, contributes to a keen sense of smell.

Evolution

part of the subfamily Capreolinae, has an evolutionary history dating back to the Pliocene-Early Pleistocene. Some scientists group them with extinct relatives in the genus Alces, while others use separate genera like Cervalces and Libralces. Libralces gallicus, an early species, was larger and had a different snout, resembling a wapiti in facial features. Cervalces latifrons, the broad-fronted stag-moose, appeared later and was the largest deer ever, with significant fossils found in Eurasia. Modern moose likely evolved from C. latifrons in East Asia during the Middle to Late Pleistocene, arriving in North America 15,000 years ago.

Subspecies

Moose, classified under various subspecies, inhabit diverse regions, each with distinct characteristics.primarily found in Eurasia and North America, with some extinct due to overhunting and habitat loss. The following are

  • 1:A. a. alces - European Elk

    Found in FINLAND, Sweden, Norway, Latvia, Estonia, and Russia, with a growing population, this mid-sized subspecies has males weighing 320 to 475 kg. Shoulder height ranges from 1.7 to 2.1 m.

  • 2:A. a. pfizenmayeri - Yakutia Elk

    Predominantly in Eastern Siberia, Mongolia, and Manchuria, this Asian subspecies is similar in size to the western moose and has a range extending from the Yenisei River to most of Siberia.

  • 3:A. a. cameloides - Ussuri Elk

    Found in the Amur-Ussuri region of far eastern Russia and northeastern China, Ussuri elk stand out with smaller antlers or no antlers at all. It is the smallest subspecies in Eurasia and the world, with a shoulder height of 1.65 to 1.85 m.

  • 4:A. a. buturlini - Chukotka Elk

    Ranging from northeastern Siberia to the Kamchatka Peninsula, this is the largest subspecies in Eurasia. Males can grow up to 2.15 m tall and weigh between 500 and 725 kg.

  • 5:A. a. americana - Eastern Moose

    Inhabiting Eastern Canada and the northeastern United States, this small-bodied subspecies has females averaging 270 kg, males averaging 365 kg, and a shoulder height of approximately 2 m.

  • 6:A. a. andersoni - Western Moose:

    Found from British Columbia to western Ontario, this middle-sized subspecies has adult females weighing 340 to 420 kg and adult males weighing 450 to 500 kg on average.

  • 7:A. a. gigas - Alaskan Moose

    In Alaska and the western Yukon, this is the largest subspecies globally, with the largest recorded individual weighing 820 kg and standing 2.33 m tall at the shoulder.

  • 8:A. a. shirasi - Shiras' Moose or Yellowstone Moose

    Occupying states like Colorado, Idaho, and Washington, this is the smallest subspecies in North America, with a weight range of 230 to 344 kg at maturity.

  • 9:A. a. caucasicus - Caucasian Elk

    Extinct due to habitat loss and overhunting in the Caucasus Mountains, its historical range included European Russia, Armenia, Georgia, Azerbaijan, and Turkey.

Diet

Moose, being herbivores, exclusively consume plants. Their sustenance primarily consists of browse, encompassing leaves and twigs from woody plants. Throughout the year, moose depend on high-quality browse from willow, aspen, birch, maple, pin cherry, and mountain ash. Balsam fir becomes particularly valuable during the winter when hardwood leaves are scarce, although moose cannot rely solely on it due to its lower nutritional value. Through selective browsing, moose actively contribute to shaping young forest stands and regulating various plant populations. Sodium holds significance in their diet, and moose incorporate aquatic plants like pondweed and water lily with higher sodium content. In areas like Maine, where natural salt licks are scarce, moose are often observed utilizing the salt runoff along roads as an artificial salt lick.

Mating Season Antlers and Breeding Rituals: During the mating season, male moose sport large, palmated antlers, showcasing dominance and offering protection during mate competition. Bulls splash their antlers with urine, emitting a scent that attracts cows for breeding. Antler Shedding and Regrowth: After the mating season, bulls shed antlers in early winter, conserving energy in the cold. In spring, antler bone covered in velvet grows rapidly, with up to 8 inches growth in nine days. Testosterone surges in September, causing velvet shedding, leaving bare antlers. Antlers in Females and Age-Related Development: Female moose rarely have antlers. Calves may develop small buds by late September, while maximum antler development in bulls is around age five. A bull's antler spread typically ranges up to 65 inches, with 55 inches considered large. Reproduction and Family Structure Rut and Calving: The breeding season (rut) occurs from late September to early October. Cows start calving at two, with most having one to two calves each May. Nutritional condition influences calving frequency. Calves stay with their mothers for a year before being driven off before the next birth. Survival and Threats Life Expectancy and Threats: On average, cows live eight years, and bulls seven, with moose rarely exceeding 20. Predation is low, but calves can be vulnerable to predators. Harvest, road kills, accidents, disease, and old age contribute to mortality, with 2,000 to 3,500 moose affected annually. Climate-Induced Threats: Changing climates expose moose to new threats. Expanding parasites and diseases, like brain worm from white-tailed deer and winter ticks, pose dangers. Winter ticks cause blood loss, hair loss, and behavioral changes, impacting Maine's moose population.

Vehicle collisions:

Moose collisions account for around 7% of Wildlife-Vehicle Collisions (WVCs) in British Columbia, posing a significant threat to motorists due to the animals' large size. Mature bull moose weigh 450-500 kgs and stand nearly 2 meters tall at the shoulder, presenting a substantial risk of injury or death in collisions. The moose's height surpasses many car hoods, leading to potential roof removal during impact. These collisions are particularly hazardous at night, as moose are challenging to see and their eyes are often above car headlights. The average cost of moose-vehicle collisions is approximately $30,760 USD, covering vehicle repairs, injuries, fatalities, towing, and other associated expenses.

Moose collisions in British Columbia peak during winter months, particularly from November to February, with the highest frequency in December and January. Winter collisions are associated with snowfall, as moose use cleared roadside areas for access to vegetation and are attracted by road salts. A smaller collision peak occurs in summer (June to August), primarily in southern regions, possibly due to last year's calves seeking territories or pregnant cows moving to calving grounds. Collisions peak from 5 pm to 12 am, with a concentration between 5 pm and 7 pm during the December peak. Northern BC, especially around Prince George, sees the majority of collisions as moose are most abundant there.
